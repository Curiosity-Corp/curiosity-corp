# Curiosity Research Corporation — Narrative Bible

**Version 1.0 — drafted 2026-06-30.** This is the single source of truth for voice, positioning, and terminology across the entire curiositycorp.org rewrite. Every content-writing agent touching any page must read this in full before writing a line of copy. If a page's existing copy conflicts with this document, this document wins.

## 1. Positioning Statement

Curiosity Research Corporation is the nonprofit research and curriculum house where AI scientists, aerospace engineers, physicists, and Silicon Valley product veterans design the pedagogy of the next century — not to teach today's curriculum better, but to invent the curriculum civilization will need next. We build the methods, the coaches, and the spaces that move a learner from first spark to provable mastery, and we do it in service of a single long arc: pushing human literacy, and human civilization, toward its next frontier.

## 2. Voice & Tone

**We sound like:** a research institute writing a grant report it's proud of, not a summer-camp brochure. Think MIT Media Lab meets a mission-driven foundation — credentialed, specific, and unafraid of big claims because every big claim is load-bearing.

**Adjectives to write toward:** visionary, precise, evidence-minded, frontier, rigorous, architectural, civilizational, credentialed, kinetic (verbs of building/igniting/steering, not "helping" and "hoping").

**Adjectives to edit out on sight:** simplistic, cozy, twee, folksy, generic-NGO, "make a difference," "give back," childlike.

### Do
- Lead with the *system* or *method* before the sentiment. ("We designed a framework that..." beats "We believe in...")
- Name the disciplines explicitly: AI, emerging tech, physics, aerospace, Silicon Valley product engineering. Specificity is what separates this from every other education nonprofit.
- Use the Kardashev scale and "next century" framing as the macro lens — but earn it with a concrete mechanism in the same paragraph or the next. Never let a civilizational claim stand alone without a program, method, or metric backing it up.
- Write sentences a program officer at NSF, DARPA, or a state Department of Education could underline and cite in a memo.
- Treat every named program (Spark Sessions, Ignite Curiosity, Curiosity Hive, etc.) as a *product* with a mechanism, not a vague "initiative."
- Use active, architectural verbs: ignite, engineer, steer, chart, compound, instrument, calibrate.

### Avoid
- Platitudes with no mechanism ("empowers individuals to participate in society," "achieve their dreams," "experience the joy of...").
- Therapy-speak and self-help diction ("inner voice," "follow your heart," "let your own light guide you"). The previous Tao Learning copy leaned hard into this — strip it out entirely.
- Lorem ipsum, unfinished sections, or placeholder stat blocks. If a number isn't real, don't imply it is — use a qualitative claim instead, or flag it for the team lead rather than inventing a figure.
- Hype with no substance ("cutting-edge," "revolutionary," "game-changing") used as a substitute for a concrete claim. If you want to say "cutting-edge," show the discipline (AI / aerospace / physics) that makes it true instead.
- Cute wordplay that undercuts credibility with a grant reviewer (bee/honey/swarm metaphors for Curiosity Hive should be used sparingly, once per page at most, and never load-bearing for the actual explanation of what the program does).

### The credibility test
Before publishing any paragraph, ask: *would a program officer at a federal grant agency read this and trust it, or would they read this and reach for a red pen?* Visionary language earns its place only when paired with a specific mechanism, discipline, or proof point in the same breath.

## 3. Messaging Pillars

### Pillar 1 — Frontier Disciplines, Not Generic STEM
**Headline:** *We don't teach the curriculum. We design the next one.*
- Our work is built by practitioners from AI/ML research, aerospace and physics labs, and award-winning Silicon Valley product teams — not curriculum vendors repackaging textbooks.
- Programs like Curiosity Coaches are built on agentic AI practice, not generic "edtech." Curiosity Camps build demonstrable capability in the same disciplines our own team works in daily.
- Every method we publish (S.P.A.R.K., Dream Quest, the Curiosity Classroom LMS) is treated as a research artifact — documented, tested, and refined the way a lab refines a protocol.

### Pillar 2 — Literacy Redefined for the Next Century
**Headline:** *Literacy is no longer reading and writing. It's AI, finance, code, and the languages civilization will run on.*
- We treat AI fluency, computational thinking, and emerging-tech literacy as core literacies — equal in priority to reading and writing, because the next century will demand them equally.
- Spark Sessions and DreamTrack instrument literacy growth the way a lab instruments an experiment: measurable, portfolio-grade, auditable.
- Our literacy advocacy isn't remedial — it's anticipatory. We teach what the workforce and the frontier will need before the demand curve catches up.

### Pillar 3 — A Pipeline to Silicon Valley and the Frontier Workforce
**Headline:** *We build the on-ramp from first curiosity to a seat in the room where the future gets built.*
- Curiosity Camps, internships, and job-shadowing exist to put students directly in front of the same caliber of company and institution our own team came from.
- DreamTrack produces auditable, portfolio-grade evidence of mastery — the credential infrastructure employers and universities can actually verify.
- We are positioned to shape not just individual outcomes but the talent pipeline feeding the industries that will define the next century: AI, aerospace, advanced computing.

### Pillar 4 — Pedagogy as Original Research
**Headline:** *We don't adopt pedagogy. We invent it.*
- The S.P.A.R.K. framework (Seek, Play, Apply, Research, Know) and the Dream Quest learning journey are original frameworks — built, tested, and iterated by our own team, not licensed from a publisher.
- Curiosity Coaches represent a genuine R&D effort in applied AI for mentorship — agentic, side-by-side, not lecture-based.
- We treat every program as a falsifiable hypothesis about how humans learn best, instrumented and revised accordingly.

### Pillar 5 — Civilizational Stakes, Kardashev Framing
**Headline:** *We measure our mission against the arc of civilization, not the school year.*
- Our north star is helping push human society toward a higher-energy, higher-capability civilization — what physicists call advancing up the Kardashev scale — by ensuring the next generation is fluent in the disciplines that get us there.
- This is not metaphor for us — our team includes people who have worked the physics and aerospace problems this framing describes. We write from inside the disciplines, not about them from a distance.
- Every program ladders up to this: literacy compounds into capability, capability compounds into a workforce, a workforce compounds into the civilizational leap.

### Pillar 6 — Infrastructure for Trust: Evidence, Audit, Accountability
**Headline:** *Every claim we make about a student, a program, or an outcome is built to survive an audit.*
- DreamTrack and the Capstone process exist specifically so funders, universities, and employers can verify outcomes — not take our word for it.
- We hold a GuideStar Platinum rating and operate as a registered 501(c)3 (EIN 33-3478603), with the financial transparency that status requires.
- This pillar exists because our primary audience — donors and grant officers — fund accountability as much as they fund vision. Pair every visionary claim with an accountability mechanism somewhere on the page.

## 4. Audience-Specific Angles

**Donors.** Speak to the donor as someone investing in a research-grade institution, not a charity case. Lead with the caliber of the team (AI, aerospace, physics, Silicon Valley) and the originality of the methods (S.P.A.R.K., Dream Quest, Curiosity Coaches) before asking for anything. Make the ask feel like joining a syndicate behind an inevitable shift in education, not rescuing an underfunded program. Always close with a concrete, auditable use of funds (a named program, a named cohort, a named tool) — never a vague "help us help kids."

**Government & grant agencies.** This audience reads for risk and verifiability before they read for vision. Front-load specificity: named frameworks, named disciplines, named outcomes infrastructure (DreamTrack, Capstone), 501(c)3/EIN boilerplate, GuideStar status. Use the Kardashev/next-century framing as the closing rationale for *why this matters at policy scale*, not as the opening hook — grant reviewers are convinced by mechanism first, vision second. Reference workforce pipeline and literacy outcomes in language that maps cleanly onto grant rubrics (STEM workforce development, literacy advancement, emerging-technology education).

**Partners (schools, businesses, NGOs, IGOs).** Speak peer-to-peer, institution-to-institution. Emphasize what Curiosity Research Corporation brings that a partner cannot build alone: original pedagogical IP (S.P.A.R.K., Dream Quest), an AI-coaching infrastructure, and a direct line into the Silicon Valley / frontier-tech talent pipeline. Frame partnership as co-investment in shared infrastructure (the Consortium, the Hive, the Coaches network) rather than a vendor relationship. For corporate partners specifically, foreground the workforce-pipeline angle — they are recruiting from what we build.

## 5. Approved Terminology & Naming Rules

### Legal vs. brand name
- **Full legal name** (use in EIN/501(c)3 statements, footers, legal pages, formal grant/donor boilerplate): **Curiosity Research Corporation**
- **Short brand** (use everywhere else — headlines, nav, body copy): **Curiosity Corporation**, or simply **Curiosity Corp** in casual/ecosystem contexts (as already established on overview.html)
- **Tagline**: **"Igniting Curiosity"** — use as the primary tagline. "Ignite Curiosity" (verb-first, imperative) is the correct form for CTAs and the program name; "Igniting Curiosity" (gerund) is correct for the brand tagline/positioning line. Do not use them interchangeably without thinking about which grammatical role you need.

### No social media presence
The organization maintains **no social media presence of any kind.** This is a firm constraint, not a style preference.
- Do not write "Follow us," "Like us," "Connect with us on," or any other social-CTA copy, anywhere on the site.
- Do not reference, link to, or imply accounts on Twitter/X, Facebook, Instagram, YouTube, LinkedIn, or any other platform — even where legacy template markup (footer icon rows, embedded timelines, "Tao Playlists" video widgets) currently has them. Those elements get removed when a page is rewritten, not relabeled.
- Footer and contact sections should route visitors to **email, the website itself, and donate/contact CTAs only.** Where current pages have a social-icon row in the footer, the rewritten footer drops it rather than re-skinning it.
- This applies even to legacy social handles that *aren't* Tao-branded (e.g., any current Curiosity-branded account) — the rule is no social presence, not just no Tao-branded social presence.

### Banned terms — remove on sight, anywhere they appear
- "Tao Learning," "Tao Learning Institute," "Tao Learning Pedagogy," "The Tao Philosophy," "Tao Playlists," "Tao Cottage," "taolearning" (in URLs, social handles, donorbox links, image alt text, Mailchimp list names) — this is the pre-rebrand identity and must not reappear anywhere, including hidden attributes, alt text, and form action URLs. (Per the no-social-media rule above, the social handles themselves should be removed outright, not just re-branded.)
- "The SMILE Method™" — retire entirely; do not replace with a new acronym unless explicitly directed. If a method name is needed, use **S.P.A.R.K.** (already established) or describe the mechanism in plain language.
- "P-20+ Initiative" — retire. If the K-through-career scope needs naming, describe it directly ("from first spark to career-ready mastery") rather than using this jargon term, which carries no brand equity and reads as bureaucratic rather than visionary.
- "Forest School pedagogy" and other unexplained pedagogy-of-the-week namedrops — do not reintroduce unless tied to a real, current program.

### Donation platform language
Donations are mid-migration: the current Donorbox links remain live temporarily while a move to a standard, Stripe-based donation platform is tracked as a separate workstream. This affects copy as follows:
- Keep all donor-facing CTAs **generic** — "Donate," "Give," "Support the Mission," "Fund the Next Frontier" — and point them at the existing donate page/link/modal already wired into the site.
- **Never name a specific donation provider in copy** (no "Donate via Donorbox," no "Powered by Stripe," etc.) anywhere on the page — not in headlines, body copy, button labels, or alt text. The backend can change without copy needing a rewrite.
- The only place a provider's own branding may legitimately appear is inside that provider's own embedded widget chrome (e.g., a Stripe card-element form), which is implementation, not copy — leave it untouched and don't write around it.

## 7. Rewrite Rubric

Apply this rubric to every page. Each content-writing agent should run through these steps in order.

### Step 1 — Hero headline + subhead pattern
- **Headline:** short (4–8 words), verb-forward or noun-forward with a frontier discipline or civilizational stake named or strongly implied. Avoid generic NGO headline patterns ("Our Mission," "What We Do") in favor of a claim. E.g., "We Engineer the Pedagogy of the Next Century" beats "Our Learning Solutions Empower Societies."
- **Subhead:** one sentence that supplies the mechanism behind the headline's claim — names a discipline, a program, or a method. The headline earns the right to be visionary; the subhead earns the right to be believed.
- Pattern: **[Visionary claim headline] → [Concrete mechanism subhead] → [CTA matched to audience]**

### Step 2 — Elevating body copy
For every paragraph of existing copy:
1. Identify the platitude or generic claim at its core (e.g., "we help students succeed").
2. Replace it with the *specific mechanism* that makes it true (a named program, a named framework, a named discipline, a named outcome).
3. If the paragraph mentions a discipline area, name it precisely (AI, not "technology"; aerospace, not "STEM"; physics, not "science").
4. Cut any sentence that exists purely for warmth with no informational content, unless it's the single emotional beat of the page (every page gets at most one or two such beats — don't stack them).
5. Remove all Lorem ipsum, "coming soon," and placeholder stat blocks. If real data doesn't exist for a claim, write the qualitative version and flag the gap to the team lead rather than inventing numbers.

### Step 3 — CTA conventions by audience
- **Donor-facing CTAs:** "Fund the Next Frontier," "Invest in Igniting Curiosity," "Back the Mission" — avoid generic "Donate Now" where a more specific alternative fits the space; keep "Donate Now" only on the actual payment modal/button itself for clarity.
- **Grant/government-facing CTAs:** "Request Our Program Brief," "Review Our Outcomes," "Partner on Policy" — these should route to or imply a credibility-first resource (briefs, data, strategy), not a generic contact form.
- **Partner-facing CTAs:** "Become a Partner," "Co-Build the Curriculum," "Join the Consortium" — emphasize collaboration framing over transaction framing.
- General rule: every CTA should make explicit *what kind of relationship* the visitor is starting, matched to their audience segment from Section 4.

### Step 4 — Before/After examples

**Example A — index.html hero line**
> **Before:** "Reading and writing empowers individuals to participate in society, achieve their dreams and to experience the joy of a good book."
> **After:** "Literacy used to mean reading and writing. We're engineering what it means next — AI fluency, computational thinking, and the languages the next century will run on."

**Example B — index.html mission paragraph**
> **Before:** "Our mission is to ignite and nurture lifelong curiosity by empowering every individual to follow their inner Curiosity Compass."
> **After:** "Our mission is to engineer the pedagogy of the next century. Built by practitioners in AI, physics, aerospace, and Silicon Valley product teams, we design the curricula, methods, and AI-driven coaching that move a learner from first spark to career-ready, auditable mastery."

**Example C — impact/mission.html opening line**
> **Before:** "We have a purpose to advance education in a P-20+ Initiative using STEAM subjects deemed necessary for individuals to join the workforce."
> **After:** "We design the literacy infrastructure for a workforce that doesn't fully exist yet — fluent in AI, emerging technology, physics, and the frontier disciplines the next century will demand."

**Example D — about/pedagogy.html opening line**
> **Before:** "We've been leaders in education and have created education systems for over 20 years where we have designed numerous methods, known as the Tao Learning Pedagogy."
> **After:** "For over 20 years, our team — drawn from AI research, aerospace engineering, and Silicon Valley product labs — has designed original pedagogical methods from first principles, refined the way a lab refines a protocol. S.P.A.R.K. and the Dream Quest journey are the result."

Use this same pattern (identify the platitude → name the mechanism → keep the sentence length disciplined) on every page in scope.

## 8. Art Direction for Imagery

**Mood:** credible-futurist. Aspirational but documentary — these should look like photos a serious research institute or aerospace lab would actually publish in an annual report, not stock-photo sci-fi. Think "real lab, real students, slightly elevated lighting" rather than "concept art."

**Subjects:** diverse students (varied age, race, gender) genuinely engaged with frontier-tech tools — AI interfaces, robotics, aerospace hardware, physics demonstration rigs, advanced computing setups. Mix individual focus shots with small-group collaborative shots. Include occasional adult mentor/coach figures shown as peers, not lecturers (consistent with the "Curiosity Coaches don't lecture" positioning).

**Settings:** modern lab and maker-space environments, clean and well-equipped but not sterile; outdoor/nature settings only when paired with visible technology (a tablet, a drone, sensors) to avoid drifting into generic "kids in a field" NGO imagery; occasional aerospace/physics-adjacent settings (observatories, fabrication spaces, mission-control-style rooms) for the higher-aspiration pages (impact, mission, partner/government pages).

**Palette:** cool, clean, slightly high-contrast — blues, teals, and whites consistent with the site's existing teal/cyan brand palette (`colors/color1.css`), with occasional warm accent lighting (amber/orange) used sparingly to suggest "spark" or "ignition" moments. Avoid washed-out, low-contrast "soft NGO" lighting.

**Lighting:** directional and a little dramatic — single strong light sources, rim lighting, screen-glow on faces when showing tech interaction. Avoid flat, even, catalog-style lighting.

**Level of futurism:** near-future, plausible, photographic — not CGI, not abstract sci-fi concept art, not over-stylized illustration. The credibility test for imagery is the same as for copy: would a grant reviewer believe this photo represents a real program?

**Example image-prompt seeds:**
1. "A diverse group of four teenage students gather around a holographic-style AI interface display in a modern maker-lab space, cool teal lighting with warm amber accent light on their faces from the screen glow, documentary photography style, shallow depth of field, aspirational but realistic."
2. "A young woman in her early twenties works alongside a small aerospace drone prototype on a clean lab workbench, physics equations visible on a whiteboard behind her, directional lighting, photojournalistic style, Silicon Valley R&D lab aesthetic."
3. "An AI coaching interface displayed on a tablet between a mentor and a student of about twelve, both leaning in with genuine engagement, modern curiosity-cottage interior with warm wood tones and cool screen light, candid documentary photography, no text overlays."
4. "Wide establishing shot of a Curiosity Camp cohort — eight diverse young adults — collaborating around laptops and a robotics kit in a bright, modern workshop space, teal and white color grading consistent with brand palette, photographic realism, slight high-contrast editorial lighting."
